## Part 1: Your Professional Introduction
 
### The Challenge
 
Create a professional introduction that:
- Shows who you are **right now**
- Hints at where you're **going**
- Demonstrates what makes you **unique**
- Positions you in the **AI + Branding space**
 
### Why This Matters
 
Your professional introduction is how you'll open every conversation:
- Networking events
- Job interviews 
- LinkedIn connections
- Cold emails
- Conference introductions
 
Most people get this wrong. They either:
- Over-explain (bore people with your life story)
- Under-explain (generic "I'm a student interested in AI")
- Miss the point (list skills without showing value)
 
**Your goal**: Create an introduction so clear and compelling that people immediately understand your value.
 
### What to Include
 
**1. Current Background**
Where are you now? What's your foundation?
 
❌ **Bad**: "I'm a graduate student at Northeastern."   
✅ **Good**: "I'm an information systems grad student with 3 years of product management experience at B2B SaaS companies."
 
**Why it works**: Specific, positions you with real experience, immediately signals your domain.
 
**2. Career Aspirations (2-3 Year Vision)**
Where are you going? What's your target?
 
❌ **Bad**: "I want to work in AI."   
✅ **Good**: "I'm positioning myself to lead AI product strategy at growth-stage startups in the marketing tech space."
 
**Why it works**: Specific role, specific type of company, specific domain. Someone can now help you.
 
**3. Why AI and Branding**
What's your unique angle?
 
❌ **Bad**: "AI is the future and branding is important."   
✅ **Good**: "I saw marketing teams spending 20 hours per week on manual social media monitoring—work AI can automate. That gap is my opportunity."
 
**Why it works**: Specific problem, clear insight, shows you understand the intersection.
 
**4. Your Unique Perspective**
What do you bring that others don't?
 
❌ **Bad**: "I'm a hard worker who learns fast."   
✅ **Good**: "I spent two years in customer success, so I build AI tools that non-technical teams actually want to use."
 
**Why it works**: Specific background, clear advantage, shows strategic thinking.
 
**5. Technical Skills (3-5)**
What can you actually do?
 
**Pro Tip**: Include both technical terms AND plain language. Why?
- **For humans**: Plain language helps anyone understand
- **For ATS systems**: Technical terms help you get found
- **For AI tools**: Both matter for search and recommendations
 
Example:
- Machine Learning (ML) - predictive modeling and pattern recognition
- Python - data analysis and automation
- ChatGPT/Claude - AI-powered content generation and workflow optimization
 
### Creating Your Visual Introduction
 
**Platform**: Figma (it's free, industry-standard, and you'll use it all semester)
 
**What Makes It Work**:
1. **Visual Hierarchy**: Most important info = biggest/first
2. **Professional Photo**: Not a selfie. Not a group photo. You, professional background, good lighting.
3. **Balanced Layout**: Don't cram. White space = professional.
4. **Brand Consistent**: Choose 2-3 colors. Stick with them. This is the start of YOUR brand.
 
**The Format**:
```
[YOUR PHOTO] [YOUR NAME] [Your One-Line Position]
Current: [Background]
Vision: [2-3 year goal]
                 
Why AI + Branding: [Your unique insight]
                 
Skills: [Skill 1] | [Skill 2] | [Skill 3]
```

**Simple answer**: An open-source AI marketing intelligence platform.
 
**What that means**: A toolkit for building AI-powered marketing tools using multiple AI agents working together.
 
**Why it matters for you**:
1. **Real-world cred**: Actual framework, actual GitHub repo, actual community
2. **Portfolio gold**: Open-source contribution = proof you can build
3. **Learning accelerator**: You'll understand AI agents, automation, and marketing tech
4. **Career positioning**: Very few students can say "I contributed to an open-source AI framework"
 
### Choosing Your Project Focus
 
You'll extend or improve one component of Madison. This semester, you're building something real.
 
**Project Selection Criteria**:
 
**1. Aligns with Your Career Goals**
If you want to work in content marketing → build content generation tools 
If you want to work in analytics → build data analysis components

Pick a project that forces you to learn your target skills.
 
**3. Solves a Real Problem**
Ask yourself: "Would a marketing team actually use this?"
 
**4. Achievable in 6 Weeks**
Be realistic. You're learning as you go.

### How to Choose Your Project
 
**Ask yourself**:
 
**1. What marketing problem frustrates you?**  
Think about inefficiencies you've seen. Manual processes. Time-consuming tasks. Decisions made on gut feeling instead of data.
 
**2. What skills do you want to showcase?**
- Technical depth? Build something with complex data processing.
- Automation? Build workflow orchestration.
- Analysis? Build intelligence/reporting tools.
- Full-stack? Build tools with user interfaces.
 
**3. What data is accessible?**
- Social media APIs (Twitter, LinkedIn have good free tiers)
- Web scraping (any public website)
- Synthetic data (you can generate test data)
- Open datasets (many marketing datasets available)
 
**4. Can you ship an MVP in 3 weeks?**  
Be honest. If it requires custom AI training, complicated infrastructure, or 10 different integrations—too big for Week 3.
 
### Writing Your Project Proposal
 
**Format**: 150-200 words
 
**Structure**:
 
**1. What will you work on?** (1-2 sentences)  
Be specific. "Improve social media analysis" is vague. "Build a competitor social monitoring tool that tracks 5 competitors' posts, identifies trending topics, and alerts when competitors post about key industry terms" is specific.
 
**2. Why is this valuable?** (2-3 sentences)  
Explain the real-world problem. Use numbers if possible. "Marketing teams spend 5-10 hours per week manually checking competitor social accounts. They miss trending topics because they can't monitor 24/7."
 
**3. What skills will you gain?** (2-3 sentences)  
List specific technical skills. "I'll learn n8n for workflow automation, Twitter and LinkedIn API integration, sentiment analysis, and automated alerting systems."
 
**4. How does this apply to marketing/branding challenges?** (2-3 sentences)  
Connect to actual marketing needs. "This addresses the challenge of competitive intelligence at scale. Marketing teams can't monitor competitors 24/7, so they miss opportunities to join conversations or respond to competitive moves."
 
**Pro Tip**: Use action verbs. You're not "exploring" or "studying"—you're developing, implementing, designing, building.
 
**Why**: When you add this to your resume, those action verbs matter.

**Quick Overview: Jungian Brand Archetypes**
 
*Note: This is just awareness-building. You'll do a deep dive in Week 7.*

- **Nike** = The Hero (achievement, challenge)

**For now**: Just be aware this framework exists. You'll use it later for positioning yourself or your Madison startup.

## Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them
 
### Pitfall #1: Generic Positioning
**Symptom**: "I'm interested in AI and branding"   
**Fix**: Be specific. What about AI? What about branding? Where do they intersect?
 
### Pitfall #2: Overrating Skills
**Symptom**: Rating yourself 4-5 on everything   
**Fix**: Be honest. Employers would rather you grow than lie.
 
### Pitfall #3: Unrealistic Goals
**Symptom**: "Build an enterprise-scale AI platform"   
**Fix**: What can you actually ship in 6 weeks while taking other classes?
 
### Pitfall #4: Disconnected Goals
**Symptom**: Goals don't relate to each other or your career   
**Fix**: Everything should point toward your 2-3 year career vision
 
### Pitfall #5: Poor Visual Presentation
**Symptom**: Messy Figma board, inconsistent formatting   
**Fix**: Remember, this IS your portfolio work. Make it professional.
 
### Pitfall #6: Trendy vs. Useful Projects
**Symptom**: "I'll build something with the latest AI model because it's cool"   
**Fix**: Build something marketing teams actually need. Useful beats trendy.
