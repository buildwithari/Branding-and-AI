# Chapter 1: Foundation Setup

## Know Yourself, Build Your Base

![Hero Image](assets/hero.png)

## What You'll Learn
 
By the end of this chapter, you will:
- Understand how to position yourself in the AI + Branding space
- Set up all the tools you need for the semester
- Choose a meaningful Madison Framework project
- Define clear, achievable goals for the next 13 weeks
- Create a professional baseline to measure your growth
- Get introduced to key branding frameworks you'll use later
 
**Time Investment**: 4-6 hours 
**Deliverable**: A comprehensive Figma board that serves as your roadmap
 
---
 
## Why This Matters
 
Most students skip the foundation work. They jump straight into building without knowing:
- What they want to achieve
- Who they're building for
- What skills they need to develop
- Where they're going
 
**That's a mistake.**
 
The best developers, designers, and brand strategists start with clarity. They know:
- Their current position
- Their target position
- The gap between the two
- How to close that gap
 
This chapter is about gaining that clarity. It's not glamorous, but it's essential.

![Clarity Image](assets/clarity.png)


## The Two Parallel Tracks
 
This course runs on two tracks:
 
### **Track 1: Madison Framework (Weeks 1-6)**
You'll master an open-source AI marketing intelligence framework and build something real with it. This is your technical credibility.
 
**What students build**: Social media scrapers, content generators, sentiment analyzers, competitor intelligence tools, synthetic persona systems, campaign automation, media monitors, and more.
 
### **Track 2: Your Brand (Weeks 7-12)**
You'll create a professional brand—personal or startup—that positions you in the market. This is your professional positioning.
 
**What you'll learn**: Brand strategy, visual identity, storytelling, portfolio development, and strategic frameworks (including Jungian archetypes for defining brand personality).
 
**Both tracks matter equally.** Technical skills without positioning = invisible talent. Positioning without skills = empty promises.

## Your Figma Board Deliverable
 
### What You're Creating
 
A single, comprehensive Figma board that serves as your semester roadmap.
 
### The Sections
 
**1. Professional Introduction**
- Visual card with your photo
- Your positioning statement
- Current background and vision
- Key skills
 
**2. Skills Inventory**
- Visual assessment of current levels
- 3 development areas with rationale
- Connection to job market research
 
**3. Madison Framework Proposal**
- Project description (what type of tool you'll build)
- Value proposition (problem it solves)
- Skills development plan
- Real-world application
 
**4. Personal Brand Baseline**
- LinkedIn screenshot
- Current state assessment
- Gap analysis
- Enhancement opportunities
 
**5. Semester Goals**
- Technical goal (Madison tool)
- Brand goal (positioning)
- Portfolio goal (showcase)
- Network goal (connections)
 
**6. Tools Setup**
- Documentation of all setups
- Screenshots
- Organized confirmation
 
### Board Standards
 
**Organization**:
- Clear section headers
- Logical flow (left to right, top to bottom)
- Related items grouped
 
**Visual Styling**:
- Consistent colors (pick 2-3)
- Consistent fonts (pick 1-2)
- Consistent spacing
- Professional appearance
 
**Professional Presentation**:
- No spelling errors
- No placeholder text
- No broken images
- Everything labeled

**Sharing**:
- Link with view/comment permissions
- Test the link before submitting
- Make sure nothing is private

## Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them
 
### Pitfall #1: Generic Positioning
**Symptom**: "I'm interested in AI and branding"   
**Fix**: Be specific. What about AI? What about branding? Where do they intersect?
 
### Pitfall #2: Overrating Skills
**Symptom**: Rating yourself 4-5 on everything   
**Fix**: Be honest. Employers would rather you grow than lie.
 
### Pitfall #3: Unrealistic Goals
**Symptom**: "Build an enterprise-scale AI platform"   
**Fix**: What can you actually ship in 6 weeks while taking other classes?
 
### Pitfall #4: Disconnected Goals
**Symptom**: Goals don't relate to each other or your career   
**Fix**: Everything should point toward your 2-3 year career vision
 
### Pitfall #5: Poor Visual Presentation
**Symptom**: Messy Figma board, inconsistent formatting
**Fix**: Remember, this IS your portfolio work. Make it professional.

### Pitfall #6: Trendy vs. Useful Projects
**Symptom**: "I'll build something with the latest AI model because it's cool"
**Fix**: Build something marketing teams actually need. Useful beats trendy.

## Pro Tips from the Instructors
 
**From Nina (40 years in creative)**:
 
> "Your introduction is your creative brief for yourself. If you can't articulate your value in 30 seconds, you'll never land the interview. Be specific. Be memorable. Be authentic. And for god's sake, use a real photo—not a cartoon avatar."
 
**From Nik (AI engineering)**:
 
> "The Madison project isn't about building the most complex thing possible. It's about building something that works and solving a real problem. Complexity impresses professors. Working solutions impress employers. Pick the project that lets you ship."
 
**On Goals**:
 
> "Most students write goals that sound good but mean nothing. 'Improve my skills' isn't a goal. 'Build three working AI tools and publish them on my portfolio site' is a goal. You'll know immediately if you achieved it or not."
 
**On Tools**:
 
> "Set up everything properly now. I've seen too many students lose work because they didn't set up version control, or waste hours because they didn't learn their tools. The first week isn't exciting, but it's essential."
 
**On Project Selection**:

> "Pick a Madison project that solves a problem you've personally experienced. If you've never worked in marketing, talk to someone who has. The best tools come from understanding real pain points, not guessing at them."

## Real Student Examples
 
### Example 1: Strong Introduction
 
**Before**:
"I'm a graduate student at Northeastern studying information systems. I'm interested in AI and marketing."
 
**After**:
"I'm an information systems grad student with 3 years of B2B SaaS product experience, positioning myself for AI product strategy roles at marketing tech companies. I'm building AI tools that solve the content distribution problem I saw at my last company—teams manually reformatting the same message for 6 different platforms. My advantage: I understand both the technical stack and the marketing team's actual workflow pain points."
 
**Why it works**: Specific background, clear target, real problem, unique advantage.
 
### Example 2: Focused Madison Project (Social Media Intelligence)
 
**Vague Project**:
"I want to work on social media analysis with Madison."
 
**Specific Project**:
"I'm building a competitor social monitoring system for Madison that tracks 5 competitors across Twitter and LinkedIn, identifies trending topics in our industry (mentions, engagement spikes, hashtag patterns), and sends daily digest reports. This solves the problem of marketing teams manually checking competitor accounts daily—a task taking 1-2 hours that could be automated. I'll develop skills in: social media API integration, trend detection algorithms, automated reporting, and n8n workflow orchestration."
 
**Why it works**: Specific tool type, clear problem, technical approach, skills development, real-world value.
 
### Example 3: Focused Madison Project (Content Generation)
 
**Vague Project**:
"I want to build a content creation tool."
 
**Specific Project**:
"I'm building a multi-platform content adapter for Madison that takes one blog post and automatically generates optimized versions for Twitter (thread), LinkedIn (professional post), and Instagram (visual + caption). This solves the content repurposing problem where marketing teams spend 30-45 minutes per platform manually reformatting. I'll develop skills in: GPT-4 prompt engineering for platform-specific adaptation, character limit handling, hashtag generation, and n8n workflow automation."
 
**Why it works**: Specific functionality, clear problem, quantified time savings, technical skills listed, achievable scope.
 
### Example 4: Focused Madison Project (Synthetic Personas)
 
**Vague Project**:
"I want to create customer personas with AI."
 
**Specific Project**:
"I'm building a synthetic persona generator for Madison that takes customer survey data and demographic information to create detailed, realistic customer personas complete with goals, pain points, preferred channels, and buying behavior. This solves the problem of marketing teams making decisions based on assumptions rather than data-driven customer understanding. I'll develop skills in: data clustering, natural language processing for survey analysis, persona template generation, and structured data visualization."
 
**Why it works**: Clear input/output, specific problem, real marketing need, technical approach defined.
 
### Example 5: Measurable Goals
 
**Vague Goals**:
- Technical: Learn Madison
- Brand: Improve LinkedIn
- Portfolio: Create portfolio
- Network: Meet people
 
**Specific Goals**:
- Technical: Deploy a working competitor social monitor processing 100+ posts daily with automated trend alerts
- Brand: Publish LinkedIn profile positioning me as "AI Marketing Technologist" with optimized headline, About section, and 3 featured projects
- Portfolio: Launch portfolio site with 3 case studies demonstrating AI + marketing automation skills, generating 50+ unique visitors
- Network: Connect with 10 marketing ops professionals at Series B+ SaaS companies, secure 3 informational interviews
 
**Why it works**: You'll know immediately if you achieved them.

## Tools & Resources
 
### For Your Introduction
- **LinkedIn Profile Examples**: Search "[Your target role] at [Target company]"
- **Positioning Resources**: "Obviously Awesome" by April Dunford
- **AI Assistance**: Ask ChatGPT to help refine your positioning statement
 
### For Skills Assessment
- **Job Search Platforms**: LinkedIn Jobs, Indeed, AngelList, YC Jobs
- **Skill Frameworks**: LinkedIn's skill assessments, Coursera's skill paths
- **Market Research**: Read job postings for 30 minutes. You'll see patterns.
 
### For Madison Project Ideas
- **Madison Repository**: https://github.com/Humanitariansai/Madison
- **Documentation**: Start with the README
- **Examples**: Look at existing components for inspiration
- **Community**: Join the Slack channel (link in Canvas)
- **Inspiration**: Browse marketing automation tools (HubSpot, Hootsuite, Buffer) to see what problems exist
 
### For Figma Board
- **Figma Templates**: Browse Figma Community for inspiration (don't copy, learn)
- **Design Principles**: Google "Visual hierarchy basics" and "Grid systems"
- **Color Tools**: Coolors.co, Adobe Color
- **Font Pairing**: Google Fonts + FontPair
